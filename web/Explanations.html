Algorithm theory

SplitUp website offers two division algorithms - "Proportional division" and "Jealousy free division".

Before we will explain how these algorithms work, we will explain in general what these concepts means and in what form the input and output are formatted.
The algorithms work based on the participants estimates of each of the items,i.e, each of the participants gives a numerical value to each of the participating items (see example below).
Once a participant has evaluated all the items – there are two options to choose from.
1) Proportional division, in which each participant receives at least 1/n of the sum of the items.
let's take an example
there are three participant's , Alice, Bob and Carol,
and the sum of all the items according to Alice's estimate is 1000 so Alice will surely get items whose sum of value according to Alice will be at least 333
- because he should get at least a third of the items.
- See a more detailed example below.
2) Division without jealousy, in which each participant receives the largest share according to his assessment. That is, if, for example, there are three participants: Alice, Bob and Carol.
Alice should get a part that, according to his own assessment will be larger than Bob's and Carol's parts.
Carol will get a part that according to his own assessment will be larger than Bob's and Alice's parts.
Bob will get a part that according to her own assessment will be larger than Carol's and Alice's parts.
In other words, everyone gets a share that in their opinion is the biggest- and so there is no jealousy in this division.
See a more detailed example below.

Some additional points

- Every division without jealousy is also a proportional division, but it does not work the other way around: not every proportional division is without jealousy.
- So why use a proportional division at all?
Because it is much faster to calculate and sometimes this condition is enough for us.
- It is important to note that if a participant gives all the items a very large value, it will not help him because the algorithm works mainly on the relationships between evaluations of the items over the years.
- And the most important point: our algorithms find divisions with an outcome of minimal sharing of the same item between people.
For example, let's assume an inheritance of several houses and we want to divide them between 4 brothers.
It would make little sense that two brothers will get the same house
(assumable one would own 70% and the other 30%).
So our algorithms find the divisions with the fewest houses divided into more than one person.
